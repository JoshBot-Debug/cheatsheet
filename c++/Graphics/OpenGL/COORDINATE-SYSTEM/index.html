Local Space​

Local space is also sometimes called Model space or Object Space

  • Local space is the coordinate system relative to an object's own origin, usually at (0,0,0).
  • The cube (or model) vertices range from (-1,-1,-1) to (1,1,1). It's origin is at (0,0,0)

Transformation:​

Local space to World space uses the Model Matrix.

vworld=Mmodel⋅vlocal\mathbf{v}_{world} = M_{model} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{local}vworld​=Mmodel​⋅vlocal​

World space to Local space uses the Inverse Model Matrix.

vlocal=Mmodel−1⋅vworld\mathbf{v}_{local}= M_{model}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{world}vlocal​=Mmodel−1​⋅vworld​

How to calculate Mmodel−1M_{model}^{-1}Mmodel−1​: An model matrix is composed of Translation (TTT), Rotation (RRR), and Scale (SSS):

Mmodel=T⋅R⋅SM_{model} = T \cdot R \cdot SMmodel​=T⋅R⋅S

To invert it analytically without doing an expensive full matrix inversion calculation, you reverse the operations and invert each individual property:

Mmodel−1=S−1⋅R−1⋅T−1M_{model}^{-1} = S^{-1} \cdot R^{-1} \cdot T^{-1}Mmodel−1​=S−1⋅R−1⋅T−1
  • T−1T^{-1}T−1 (Inverse Translation): Negate the translation values (move backward instead of forward).
  • R−1R^{-1}R−1 (Inverse Rotation): Transpose the rotation matrix (since pure rotation matrices are orthogonal, their transpose equals their inverse), or invert the quaternion.
  • S−1S^{-1}S−1 (Inverse Scale): Reciprocate the scale factors (divide by the scale instead of multiplying, e.g., 1/scalex1/scale_x1/scalex​).

World space​

World space places all objects into a common coordinate system. All objects relative to world origin.

Cube local vertex = (1,1,1)
Cube position in world = (5,0,0)
Cube world space = (6,1,1)

Transformation:​

World space to View space uses the View Matrix.

vview=Mview⋅vworld\mathbf{v}_{view} = M_{view} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{world}vview​=Mview​⋅vworld​

View space to World space uses the Inverse View Matrix.

vworld=Mview−1⋅vview\mathbf{v}_{world} = M_{view}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{view}vworld​=Mview−1​⋅vview​

View Space​

In view space, coordinates are relative to the camera.

The camera is treated as being at: (0,0,0) looking along a standard direction (often −Z in OpenGL-style conventions).

World:

Camera = (0,0,10)
Object = (0,0,0)

View space:

Camera = (0,0,0)
Object = (0,0,-10)

Everything is transformed so the camera becomes the origin.

Transformation:​

View space to Clip space uses the Projection Matrix.

vclip=Mproj⋅vview\mathbf{v}_{clip} = M_{proj} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{view}vclip​=Mproj​⋅vview​

Clip space to View space uses the Inverse Projection Matrix.

vview=Mproj−1⋅vclip\mathbf{v}_{view} = M_{proj}^{-1} \cdot \mathbf{v}_{clip}vview​=Mproj−1​⋅vclip​

Clip Space​

A specialized space where coordinates are evaluated to see if they are visible on screen. If a vertex lies outside the boundaries defined by its www component (i.e., −w≤x,y,z≤w-w \le x, y, z \le w−w≤x,y,z≤w), it is "clipped" (discarded).

Transformation:​

Clip space to NDC (Normalized Device Coordinates) Perspective Divide.

vndc=[x/wy/wz/w]\mathbf{v}_{ndc} = \begin{bmatrix} x/w \\ y/w \\ z/w \end{bmatrix}vndc​=​x/wy/wz/w​​

NDC to Clip space Perspective Multiply.

vclip=[nx⋅wny⋅wnz⋅w]\mathbf{v}_{clip} = \begin{bmatrix} nx \cdot w \\ ny \cdot w \\ nz \cdot w \end{bmatrix}vclip​=​nx⋅wny⋅wnz⋅w​​

You've done this or copied it a million times:

vec4 clipPosition = projection * view * model * vec4(vPosition, 1.0);
vec3 ndc = clipPosition.xyz / clipPosition.w;

Screen Space​

The actual pixel coordinates of your monitor/window (e.g., from 0 to 1920 on the X-axis, and 0 to 1080 on the Y-axis).

  • Viewport transform is implemented as a scale-and-bias shift to map the [−1,1][-1, 1][−1,1] NDC cube to [0,width][0, \text{width}][0,width] and [0,height][0, \text{height}][0,height]
